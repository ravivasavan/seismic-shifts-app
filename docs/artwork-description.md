# Seismic Shifts — Artwork Description

A diptych. A digital trace listens to the room and draws its acoustic
energy as a single, slowly scrolling line. A hand-drawn companion on
A0 paper sets that small reading inside a much longer arc — 65,000
years of Aboriginal sign languages, pockmarked by the seismic moments
that have shaped and unmade Deaf life and signed languages.

The two pieces are seismograms at vastly different scales: one room
across an afternoon, one civilisation across deep time.

A digital companion to the long-arc piece lives on the project site
at [/arc.html](https://ravivasavan.github.io/seismic-shifts-site/arc.html).
It renders the same arc as the eventual hand-drawn A0 ink companion,
in landscape, with the right ~10% of the page intentionally left
blank — the unwritten near future.

## Catalogue entry (~250 words)

**Seismic Shifts** (2026)
Ravi Vasavan
Software, microphone, screen; ink on A0 paper.

The work is a two-part seismogram. A digital trace listens to the
room and draws its acoustic energy as a single, slowly scrolling line
— one mic, one line, one slow scroll. A hand-drawn companion piece
sets that small reading inside a much longer arc: 65,000 years of
Aboriginal sign languages, including Warlpiri, Yolngu, Arandic and
others, pockmarked by the seismic moments that have shaped — and
unmade — Deaf life and signed languages.

That longer trace runs from the deep continuity of Aboriginal sign
languages, through the years before the 1880 Milan Congress, the
suppression of signed languages in deaf education that followed, and
the eugenics of Alexander Graham Bell and his contemporaries. Across
that arc, Deaf existence and signed languages plummet below the
historical baseline and have not, even now, recovered.

The claim is that what is recorded is real: a room has presence, with
or without anyone to hear or see it; a language has presence, with or
without an institution willing to recognise it. The piece is for both
Deaf and hearing audiences.

No audio is captured, stored, or recoverable. Only one number per
second — the room's acoustic shape over time — drives the digital
trace. It is equivalent to a barometer recording air pressure.

## Timeline — the long arc

The hand-drawn companion piece traces the presence of Deaf life and
signed languages across deep time. Below baseline = suppressed; at
baseline = sustained as a natural feature of human community; above
baseline = institutionally recognised. The shape of the trace is the
argument of the work.

### Deep continuity — c. 65,000 BP – 1500 CE

- **c. 65,000 BP onwards** — Aboriginal Australian presence on the
  continent. Aboriginal sign languages develop and are transmitted
  across millennia. Named systems documented by linguistics include
  **Warlpiri Sign Language** (Central Australia), **Yolngu Sign
  Language** (Northeast Arnhem Land), and signing traditions among
  **Arandic**, **Pitjantjatjara**, **Ngada**, and **Western Desert**
  peoples. Used in mourning rituals, hunting, ceremony, and for
  communication across language groups.
- **Deep past, other continents** — **Plains Indian Sign Language**
  ("Hand Talk") functions as a lingua franca across many North
  American Plains nations. Signing communities are also documented
  in Mediterranean, African, and Asian contexts, with less continuous
  written record.
- **Throughout** — Deaf life and signed languages exist as a natural
  feature of human community. The trace runs at baseline.

### Early documentation — 1500s – 1750

- **c. 1550** — Pedro Ponce de León, Spain, teaches deaf children of
  nobility; first documented European deaf education.
- **1620** — Juan Pablo Bonet, *Reducción de las letras y arte para
  enseñar a hablar a los mudos*; first treatise on deaf education and
  the manual alphabet.

### Western institutional emergence — 1750 – 1880

- **1755** — Abbé de l'Épée founds the first free school for the deaf
  in Paris and embraces French Sign Language (LSF) as the language of
  instruction.
- **1760** — Thomas Braidwood opens the first British school for the
  deaf in Edinburgh.
- **1817** — American School for the Deaf founded in Hartford,
  Connecticut, by Thomas Hopkins Gallaudet and Laurent Clerc. ASL
  emerges from a fusion of LSF, Martha's Vineyard Sign Language, and
  home signs.
- **1864** — Gallaudet College (now University) chartered in
  Washington, DC.

### The break — 1880

- **1880** — **Second International Congress on Education of the
  Deaf, Milan.** 164 delegates, almost all hearing, vote to ban sign
  languages from deaf education in favour of oralism. The resolutions
  ripple across most of the West for nearly a century. The trace
  plummets.

### Eugenics era — 1880 – 1945

- **1883** — **Alexander Graham Bell** reads *Memoir Upon the
  Formation of a Deaf Variety of the Human Race* before the National
  Academy of Sciences. The text argues against deaf-deaf marriage,
  against deaf residential schools, and against the use of sign
  languages. Published 1884.
- **1880s onwards** — Oralism dominates deaf education globally.
  Children punished for signing in classrooms.
- **1907** — Indiana enacts the first US compulsory sterilisation
  law. Similar laws follow in 32 states; deaf people are among those
  targeted.
- **Late 19th – mid 20th century** — Australia's assimilation
  policies and removal practices target Aboriginal communities and
  their languages, including Aboriginal sign languages.
- **1933** — Nazi Germany's *Law for the Prevention of Hereditarily
  Diseased Offspring* authorises forced sterilisation. Roughly 17,000
  deaf people are sterilised under this and related laws.
- **1939 – 1945** — Aktion T4 and related programs murder thousands
  of deaf and disabled people.

### Slow recovery — 1960 – present

- **1960** — William Stokoe, *Sign Language Structure*: ASL
  demonstrated as a fully grammatical natural language.
- **1965** — Stokoe's *Dictionary of American Sign Language on
  Linguistic Principles*.
- **1975** — *Auslan* named and described as a distinct language
  (Trevor Johnston and others).
- **1988** — Deaf President Now protests at Gallaudet University
  install I. King Jordan as first deaf president.
- **1995** — Auslan recognised in Australia's national language
  policy.
- **2003** — New Zealand Sign Language recognised as an official
  language of New Zealand.
- **2010** — International Congress on Education of the Deaf issues
  *Statement of Principle and Accord for the Future*, formally
  rejecting and apologising for the 1880 Milan resolutions.
- **2020s** — Ongoing recognition and documentation of Aboriginal
  sign language traditions in linguistic and cultural scholarship;
  continuing work on Yolngu, Warlpiri and other Aboriginal sign
  systems.

### Today — 2026

Deaf existence and signed languages have not recovered to their
pre-1880 baseline. Aboriginal sign languages persist in some
communities but with reduced inter-generational transmission.
Cochlear-implant policy debates continue. Recognition is uneven
across jurisdictions.

The trace is rising. It is not yet at the line it left.

## Notes on the diptych

- **Scale.** The digital trace covers minutes to days; the drawing
  covers tens of thousands of years. The cream/ink palette is shared.
- **The orange chip.** In the digital piece, a quiet orange marks the
  active 15-minute window. On the drawing, the same orange bands the
  eugenics era — 1880 to 1945. Same visual language, different time
  scales.
- **The red mark.** In the digital piece, a single red dot signals
  session start — the work coming into the room. On the drawing, the
  same red marks 1880 — the moment that broke the historical line.
- **What baseline means.** Baseline on the long arc is the deep
  historical norm: 65,000 years of continuous signed-language
  presence in human community. Recognition by Western institutions
  is incidental to that baseline, not the source of it.
